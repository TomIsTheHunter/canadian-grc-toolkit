# Resources — Master Index and Regulatory Reference Library

This folder contains individual reference documents for every regulatory framework, federal and provincial legislation, and industry standard relevant to FRFI cybersecurity and privacy compliance in Canada. Each document follows a standardised structure: a YAML metadata block, substantive coverage sections, an explicit **Relevance to OSFI 24-Hour Incident Reporting** section, and cross-references to related documents in this library.

## Relationship to the Primary Reference Document

This folder directly supports the primary operational reference for OSFI incident reporting:

> **[`../OSFI_Incident_Reporting_Trigger_Criteria.md`](../OSFI_Incident_Reporting_Trigger_Criteria.md)**

That document provides the authoritative list of trigger criteria (Groups A, B, and C), the 24-hour regulatory reporting window, the recommended 12-hour internal escalation threshold, the reporting procedure and form requirements, and a cross-framework mapping table that links every trigger criterion back to the source documents held in this folder. The resources here provide the full statutory and regulatory context behind each criterion — enabling compliance teams, legal counsel, and internal auditors to trace any reporting obligation back to its primary source.

## Mandatory vs. Voluntary Summary

### Mandatory Regulatory Obligations

The following documents represent binding legal or regulatory obligations. Non-compliance may result in regulatory enforcement, financial penalties, supervisory intervention, or reputational consequences.

#### Mandatory – FRFI *(applies to all Federally Regulated Financial Institutions)*

- **OSFI Technology and Cybersecurity Incident Reporting Advisory** — The primary source of the 24-hour incident reporting obligation; establishes trigger criteria, notification timelines, and reporting contacts.
- **OSFI Guideline B-13 (Technology and Cyber Risk Management)** — Mandatory technology and cyber risk governance framework for all FRFIs; establishes the broader security programme requirements within which incident reporting sits.
- **OSFI Guideline B-10 (Third-Party Risk Management)** — Mandatory third-party and outsourcing risk framework; third-party cyber incidents that affect FRFIs are a direct OSFI reporting trigger.
- **OSFI Guideline E-21 (Operational Risk and Resilience Management)** — Mandatory operational risk and resilience standards; operational disruptions meeting threshold criteria are OSFI reporting triggers.
- **OSFI Guideline E-23 (Model Risk Management)** — Mandatory model risk governance; material failures in model-driven risk systems may constitute OSFI-reportable technology incidents.
- **OSFI Integrity and Security Guideline** — Mandatory guideline covering threats to FRFI integrity from malicious actors; threats to integrity of senior personnel or critical systems are a distinct OSFI reporting trigger category.
- **OSFI Corporate Governance Guideline** — Mandatory governance framework; the Board and senior leadership notification obligations during a material incident are grounded in this guideline.

#### Mandatory – Federal *(applies broadly under federal law; includes FRFIs)*

- **PIPEDA — Breach of Security Safeguards Regulations (SOR/2018-64)** — Federally mandated breach notification obligations; requires notification to the Office of the Privacy Commissioner of Canada (OPC) and affected individuals when a breach creates a real risk of significant harm. OPC notification is itself an OSFI Advisory trigger criterion.
- **Bill C-26 — Critical Cyber Systems Protection Act (CCSPA)** ⚠️ — *Proposed legislation; not yet in force as of April 2, 2026. Verify current legislative status.* If enacted, would impose mandatory cybersecurity programmes, supply chain security obligations, and cyber incident reporting to the Communications Security Establishment (CSE) for designated critical infrastructure operators, including large FRFIs.
- **Bill C-27 — Digital Charter Implementation Act, 2022 (CPPA / AIDA)** ⚠️ — *Proposed legislation; not yet in force as of April 2, 2026. Verify current legislative status.* If enacted, would replace PIPEDA with the Consumer Privacy Protection Act and introduce the Artificial Intelligence and Data Act; would tighten breach notification requirements and introduce AI governance obligations.

#### Mandatory – Quebec *(applies to any organisation handling personal information of Quebec residents)*

- **Quebec Law 25 — Act to Modernise Privacy Legislation (ARPPIPS)** — In full force as of September 22, 2023. Imposes breach notification obligations to the Commission d'accès à l'information (CAI) and affected individuals within 72 hours of awareness of a confidentiality incident; directly relevant to FRFIs with Quebec operations or Quebec-resident customers.

### Voluntary / Best Practice Frameworks

The following documents represent internationally recognised standards and frameworks that are not legally required in Canada but are frequently referenced by regulators (including OSFI) as benchmarks for sound practice. Adoption may support demonstrated compliance with mandatory obligations.

- **ISO/IEC 27001:2022 (Information Security Management Systems)** — Certifiable international standard; certification provides a structured, auditable evidence base that supports OSFI B-13 and CCSPA Cybersecurity Programme compliance. OSFI has referenced ISO 27001 as an acceptable ISMS framework.
- **ISO/IEC 27002:2022 (Information Security Controls)** — Companion guidance standard to ISO 27001; provides implementation guidance for 93 security controls organised into four themes. Controls map closely to OSFI B-13 technology and cyber risk domains.
- **NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0 (CSF 2.0)** — Widely adopted framework organised around six functions: Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, and Recover. Referenced in OSFI B-13 guidance; useful for structuring CCSPA Cybersecurity Programme requirements and for mapping cyber maturity against regulatory expectations.
- **SOC 2 Trust Services Criteria — CC6 (Logical and Physical Access Controls)** — Voluntary audit and assurance framework; SOC 2 Type II reports from third-party service providers are a key input to OSFI B-10 third-party due diligence and ongoing monitoring obligations.

## Key Interactions Between Mandatory Regulations

The mandatory regulatory obligations in this library do not operate in isolation — they form an interlocking web that may be triggered simultaneously by a single cyber or privacy incident. Compliance teams must have integrated incident response processes capable of addressing all concurrent obligations.

### Incident Cascade: How One Event Triggers Multiple Regimes

**1. OSFI Incident Reporting Advisory (24-hour window)**
The OSFI Advisory is the primary incident reporting obligation for FRFIs. A qualifying incident must be reported to OSFI's Technology Risk Division (TRD) and the Lead Supervisor within 24 hours. What triggers the OSFI Advisory also frequently triggers obligations under other regimes — and in several cases, activation of another regime is itself an OSFI Advisory trigger.

**2. PIPEDA DERR → OPC Notification (concurrent with OSFI)**
If the cyber incident involves unauthorised access to, or loss of, personal information in a manner that creates a real risk of significant harm to an individual, the FRFI must notify the Office of the Privacy Commissioner (OPC) and affected individuals under the PIPEDA Breach of Security Safeguards Regulations. Critically, the OSFI Advisory explicitly identifies OPC notification as a trigger criterion (Group C) — meaning the existence of a PIPEDA reporting obligation itself triggers OSFI reporting, and vice versa. These two obligations are mutually reinforcing and must be satisfied simultaneously.

**3. Quebec Law 25 → CAI Notification (concurrent, different timeline)**
If the same incident involves a confidentiality breach affecting Quebec residents' personal information, the FRFI must separately notify the Commission d'accès à l'information (CAI) within 72 hours of awareness. Quebec Law 25 obligations run concurrently with PIPEDA DERR obligations — the same incident may require coordinated notifications to two separate regulators (OPC and CAI) on potentially different timelines. FRFIs with Quebec customers must treat these as parallel workstreams, not sequential ones.

**4. Corporate Governance Guideline → Board Escalation (concurrent OSFI trigger)**
Material cyber incidents require prompt escalation to the Board of Directors and relevant Board committees under the OSFI Corporate Governance Guideline. This Board notification obligation is simultaneously an OSFI Advisory reporting trigger (Group C criteria): incidents that require Board-level escalation under governance expectations are, by that fact, OSFI-reportable. FRFIs must ensure that internal Board escalation policies are calibrated to meet — not exceed — the 24-hour regulatory window.

**5. OSFI B-10 → Third-Party Incident Reporting**
When a third-party vendor, outsourcing provider, or critical service supplier suffers a cyber incident that affects the FRFI, OSFI B-10 third-party risk management obligations require assessment and documented response. If the third-party incident meets OSFI Advisory trigger criteria (Group B5: disaster declaration by a third-party vendor; Group B6: third-party incident materially impacting the FRFI), an OSFI Advisory report is required concurrently. Third-party incidents involving personal data may additionally trigger PIPEDA DERR and/or Quebec Law 25 notifications.

**6. OSFI E-21 → Operational Disruption Thresholds**
Operational disruptions (e.g., prolonged system unavailability, activation of Disaster Recovery plans) trigger both OSFI E-21 resilience obligations and OSFI Advisory Group B criteria. The disruption thresholds in E-21 (e.g., failure to meet recovery time objectives for critical operations) are aligned with but not identical to the Advisory trigger criteria — a disruption may satisfy one set of thresholds without the other. FRFIs must evaluate both sets of criteria independently and concurrently for every operational disruption.

**7. Bill C-26 / CCSPA → Parallel Federal Cyber Reporting *(if enacted)***
⚠️ *Legislative status unconfirmed as of April 2, 2026 — verify before relying on this section.*
If Bill C-26 is enacted, designated FRFIs will face a second federal cyber incident reporting obligation running concurrently with the OSFI Advisory: prescribed cybersecurity incidents must be reported to the Communications Security Establishment (CSE) and to OSFI in its separate capacity as the CCSPA sector regulator. Early consultation materials indicated a CCSPA reporting window potentially shorter than 24 hours for serious incidents affecting critical cyber systems. This would require FRFIs to maintain internal escalation processes capable of satisfying the stricter CCSPA timeline while simultaneously meeting the OSFI Advisory 24-hour requirement — potentially delivering two separate notifications to OSFI in different regulatory capacities.

## Standard Document Structure

Every resource document in this folder follows the same structure to ensure consistency and navigability:

1. **YAML Metadata block** — Document name, issuer, effective date, last verified date, status, type, and official URL
2. **Overview** — Purpose, scope, and applicability
3. **Key Provisions / Coverage** — Substantive sections covering the document's material requirements
4. **Relevance to OSFI 24-Hour Incident Reporting** — An explicit section mapping the document's provisions to the OSFI Advisory trigger criteria
5. **Cross-References** — Links to related documents within this library
