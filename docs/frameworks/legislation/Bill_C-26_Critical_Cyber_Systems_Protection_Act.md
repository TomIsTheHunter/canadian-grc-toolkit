## 1. Overview — Two Parts

Bill C-26 has **two distinct parts**:

| Part       | Description                                                                                                                                                                  |
|------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Part 1** | Amends the *Telecommunications Act* to authorise the government to direct telecommunications service providers (TSPs) to take security measures                              |
| **Part 2** | Enacts the **Critical Cyber Systems Protection Act (CCSPA)** — a new statute creating mandatory cybersecurity obligations for designated operators of critical cyber systems |

The CCSPA is the primary piece of new legislation and has direct implications for FRFIs that are designated as operators of **critical cyber systems** in the **banking and financial sector**.

---

## 2. Part 1 — Telecommunications Act Amendments

### Key Changes to the *Telecommunications Act*

- Authorises the **Governor in Council** to issue orders requiring TSPs to implement security measures to protect networks from threats
- Authorises the government to direct TSPs to **prohibit or restrict** use of specific equipment or services (e.g., equipment from particular vendors)
- Includes **confidentiality provisions** preventing TSPs from disclosing orders received

### Relevance to FRFIs

FRFIs that operate telecommunications infrastructure or rely heavily on specific TSPs may be indirectly affected by Part 1 orders — particularly if a vendor or infrastructure provider is directed to take action that affects FRFI connectivity.

## 3. Part 2 — Critical Cyber Systems Protection Act (CCSPA)

### 3.1 Purpose

The CCSPA creates a **mandatory cybersecurity regime** for operators of critical cyber systems in designated sectors. Its purpose is to ensure that Canada's most critical infrastructure — including the financial sector — has minimum cybersecurity standards and can rapidly report and respond to cyber incidents.

### 3.2 Core Obligations for Designated Operators

| Obligation                               | Description                                                                                                         |
|------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Establish a cybersecurity programme**  | Documented, implemented programme to protect critical cyber systems                                                 |
| **Mitigate supply chain risks**          | Assess and mitigate risks from third-party suppliers to critical cyber systems                                      |
| **Report cybersecurity incidents**       | Report prescribed cybersecurity incidents to the Communications Security Establishment (CSE) and relevant regulator |
| **Report supply chain incidents**        | Report incidents affecting the supply chain of critical cyber systems                                               |
| **Comply with cybersecurity directions** | Comply with directions issued by the Governor in Council or designated authority                                    |
| **Maintain records**                     | Maintain records of cybersecurity programmes and incidents                                                          |

## 4. Designated Operators and Covered Sectors

### 4.1 Designated Sectors

The CCSPA applies to operators of critical cyber systems in the following sectors:

| Sector                 | Regulator                                                          |
|------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Finance**            | Office of the Superintendent of Financial Institutions (OSFI)      |
| **Telecommunications** | Canadian Radio-television and Telecommunications Commission (CRTC) |
| **Energy**             | Canada Energy Regulator (CER)                                      |
| **Transportation**     | Transport Canada                                                   |
| **Nuclear**            | Canadian Nuclear Safety Commission (CNSC)                          |

### 4.2 Who is a "Designated Operator"?

Not all entities in the designated sectors are covered — only those **designated by the Governor in Council** as operators of critical cyber systems. In the financial sector:

- Large banks and insurance companies are the most likely candidates for designation
- Designation criteria focus on systemic importance and interconnectedness
- Smaller FRFIs may not be designated but should monitor for updates

### 4.3 Critical Cyber Systems

A **critical cyber system** under CCSPA is a system that, if compromised, could:
- Endanger the safety of Canadians
- Damage the Canadian economy or financial system
- Undermine the security of Canada

---

## 5. Mandatory Cybersecurity Programmes

### 5.1 Programme Requirements

Designated operators must establish and implement a **Cybersecurity Programme** that:

- Identifies and inventories critical cyber systems
- Assesses risks to those systems
- Implements controls proportionate to risk
- Addresses supply chain and third-party risks
- Tests and validates security controls
- Maintains incident detection and response capabilities
- Trains personnel on cybersecurity

### 5.2 Programme Documentation

The Cybersecurity Programme must be:
- Documented and maintained
- Reviewed at least annually or following material changes
- Available for inspection by the designated regulator (OSFI for financial sector)

### 5.3 Supply Chain Risk Management

A distinctive feature of CCSPA is the **explicit supply chain security requirement**:
- Designated operators must assess risks from third-party suppliers to critical cyber systems
- This requirement parallels OSFI B-10 third-party risk management but is specifically focused on the security of the supply chain for critical systems
- Operators must mitigate identified supply chain risks

## 6. Incident Reporting to the Communications Security Establishment (CSE)

### 6.1 Reporting Obligation

Under the CCSPA, designated operators must report **prescribed cybersecurity incidents** to:

1. The **Communications Security Establishment (CSE)** — Canada's national cybersecurity agency
2. Their **sector regulator** — for FRFIs, this is **OSFI**

### 6.2 What Must Be Reported

Prescribed incidents are defined by regulation and are expected to cover:
- Incidents with a serious impact on critical cyber systems
- Incidents that may affect national security, public safety, or the Canadian economy
- Supply chain incidents affecting the security of critical cyber systems

### 6.3 Reporting Timeline

The specific reporting timeline for CCSPA will be set by regulation. Early consultation documents indicated a window that may be **shorter than OSFI's 24-hour window** for certain critical incidents. The exact timeline will be established in the regulations.

### 6.4 CSE's Role

The **Communications Security Establishment (CSE)** serves as the central repository for critical cyber incident reports from across all designated sectors. CSE:
- Analyses incident data for threat intelligence
- Shares information (at appropriate classification levels) to support sector-wide defence
- Coordinates with sector regulators

---

## 7. Government Cybersecurity Directions

### 7.1 Cybersecurity Directions

The CCSPA authorises the **Governor in Council** to issue **cybersecurity directions** to designated operators requiring them to take (or refrain from taking) specific cybersecurity actions.

Directions may:
- Require specific security measures
- Mandate the removal of particular equipment or software from critical systems
- Restrict use of services from specific vendors or jurisdictions

### 7.2 Confidentiality of Directions

Like Part 1, CCSPA directions may be accompanied by confidentiality requirements preventing operators from disclosing that they have received a direction.

### 7.3 Compliance Obligations

Designated operators must comply with directions promptly. Non-compliance can result in penalties. Compliance with a direction may have operational impacts on critical systems — which themselves may trigger OSFI incident reporting.

## 8. FRFIs as Critical Infrastructure Operators

### 8.1 The Finance Sector Under CCSPA

FRFIs designated as operators of critical cyber systems under CCSPA will face a **dual regulatory regime**:

| Regulator                          | Obligation                                                                        |
|------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **OSFI**                           | Ongoing supervisory expectations (B-13, B-10, E-21, I&S, Incident Advisory)       |
| **OSFI as CCSPA sector regulator** | CCSPA Cybersecurity Programme oversight; CCSPA incident report receipt and review |
| **CSE**                            | CCSPA cyber incident reports; threat intelligence sharing                         |

### 8.2 OSFI as Both Sector Regulator and Prudential Supervisor

A significant feature of the CCSPA financial sector regime is that **OSFI serves as both the prudential supervisor and the CCSPA sector regulator**. This creates an integrated oversight relationship where CCSPA compliance is monitored alongside existing OSFI guideline compliance.

### 8.3 Coordination of Reporting Obligations

For designated FRFIs, a cyber incident affecting a critical cyber system may require reporting under:
1. OSFI Technology and Cybersecurity Incident Reporting Advisory (24 hours to OSFI/Lead Supervisor)
2. CCSPA (to CSE and OSFI as sector regulator, within prescribed timeline)

FRFIs must build integrated incident response and reporting processes that address both obligations simultaneously.

## 9. Relevance to OSFI 24-Hour Incident Reporting

### 9.1 Parallel and Concurrent Reporting Obligations

For FRFIs designated as operators of critical cyber systems under CCSPA, incidents affecting critical cyber systems will likely trigger **simultaneous reporting obligations** under:
- The OSFI Advisory (24-hour window to OSFI TRD and Lead Supervisor)
- CCSPA (within the prescribed regulatory timeline to CSE and OSFI as sector regulator)

### 9.2 CCSPA Reporting May Be Stricter

Early consultation on CCSPA indicated that the prescribed incident reporting timeline may be **shorter than 24 hours** for serious incidents affecting critical cyber systems. If enacted, FRFIs would need to maintain internal escalation and notification processes capable of meeting the more stringent CCSPA timeline while also satisfying the OSFI Advisory 24-hour requirement.

> 📌 **Implication:** If CCSPA is enacted with a shorter reporting window (e.g., 6–12 hours), the internal organisational 12-hour threshold currently recommended as best practice would need to be re-examined and potentially tightened.

### 9.3 Supply Chain Incidents as OSFI Advisory Triggers

CCSPA's supply chain incident reporting obligation aligns with OSFI Advisory Group B criteria (third-party incidents). A supply chain cyber incident affecting a FRFI's critical cyber system would likely trigger:
- CCSPA reporting to CSE and OSFI (as sector regulator)
- OSFI Advisory Criterion B5 (disaster declaration by third-party vendor) or B6 (impact to third party affecting the FRFI)

### 9.4 Government Directions May Cause Reportable Disruptions

Compliance with a CCSPA government direction (e.g., removal of equipment from critical systems) may itself cause a disruption that:
- Triggers the FRFI's DR plan (Advisory Criterion B4)
- Causes operational impact to critical systems (Criterion A6)
- Requires Board/Exec notification (Criterion C2)

FRFIs should include government direction compliance scenarios in their incident response planning.

> 📌 **Summary:** Bill C-26/CCSPA, if enacted, will create a parallel federal cyber incident reporting regime for designated financial sector operators. For affected FRFIs, cyber incidents will require simultaneous reporting to OSFI (under the Advisory) and to CSE/OSFI (under CCSPA). FRFIs must monitor legislative developments and prepare integrated, multi-regulator incident notification processes.
