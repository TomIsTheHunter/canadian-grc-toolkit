## 1. Overview

The **Breach of Security Safeguards Regulations (SOR/2018-64)** were enacted under PIPEDA to establish mandatory breach reporting, notification, and record-keeping obligations for private-sector organisations, including FRFIs.

Prior to these regulations, breach reporting under PIPEDA was voluntary. The 2018 regulations created a **mandatory regime** requiring organisations to:
1. Report breaches that create a "real risk of significant harm" to the OPC
2. Notify affected individuals of such breaches
3. Maintain records of **all** breaches (regardless of harm threshold)

These obligations run **concurrently** with OSFI's incident reporting requirements for FRFIs — both may be triggered by the same incident.

---

## 2. What Triggers Breach Reporting — "Real Risk of Significant Harm"

### 2.1 The Threshold

A breach of security safeguards must be reported to the OPC and affected individuals if it is **reasonable to believe that the breach creates a real risk of significant harm** to one or more individuals.

### 2.2 Definition of "Breach of Security Safeguards"

A breach occurs when there is a **loss of, unauthorised access to, or unauthorised disclosure of personal information** as a result of:
- A failure of physical, organisational, or technical safeguards

This includes:
- Lost or stolen devices/media containing personal information
- Ransomware attacks encrypting personal data
- Unauthorised access by employees or third parties
- Accidental disclosure of personal data

### 2.3 Definition of "Significant Harm"

PIPEDA defines **significant harm** to include bodily harm, humiliation, financial loss, identity theft, negative effects on the credit record, and damage to relationships or employment prospects.

---

## 3. Factors for Assessing Real Risk of Significant Harm

Organisations must assess the following factors when determining whether a breach creates a real risk of significant harm:

| Factor                                               | Considerations                                                                                                            |
|------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Sensitivity of the personal information**          | Financial information, health data, SIN, passwords, and account credentials are inherently more sensitive                 |
| **Probability that the information will be misused** | Was it accessed by a sophisticated actor? Is it on the dark web? Was it encrypted?                                        |
| **Number of individuals affected**                   | Larger scale breaches may have greater harm potential; but small-scale breaches of highly sensitive data may also trigger |
| **Nature of the unauthorised access**                | Deliberate exfiltration vs. accidental exposure vs. lost unencrypted device                                               |
| **Whether the organisation can prevent harm**        | Was the data recoverable before misuse? Was the recipient bound by confidentiality?                                       |

### Practical Assessment Approach

FRFIs should use a documented **breach severity assessment matrix** that maps these factors to a harm determination. This matrix should be part of the Incident Response Plan and should be applied consistently within the first hours of identifying a potential breach.

## 4. Reporting to the Office of the Privacy Commissioner (OPC)

### 4.1 When to Report

As soon as the organisation determines that a breach has occurred **and** that it creates a real risk of significant harm, it must report to the OPC. There is **no explicit deadline** in the Regulations, but the OPC expects reporting **as soon as feasible** — in practice, this means within days of the determination, not weeks.

### 4.2 Form of Report

Reports to the OPC must include:

| Required Information               | Description                                                          |
|------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Date/period of breach**          | When the breach occurred (or estimated period)                       |
| **Description of circumstances**   | How the breach occurred                                              |
| **Nature of information involved** | Type and sensitivity of personal information affected                |
| **Number of individuals affected** | Actual or estimated number                                           |
| **Steps taken to reduce harm**     | Mitigation actions (e.g., device recovery, account lockdown)         |
| **Notification status**            | Whether affected individuals have been or will be notified, and when |
| **Contact information**            | Organisation's designated privacy contact                            |

### 4.3 OPC Reporting Portal

The OPC maintains an online portal for breach reporting. FRFIs should ensure their incident response team has access to the portal and designated contacts who can submit reports.

## 5. Notification to Affected Individuals

### 5.1 When Notification Is Required

Individuals must be notified of a breach when:
- A real risk of significant harm exists (same threshold as OPC reporting)
- The notification must be direct (not just a press release or website notice)

### 5.2 Content of Individual Notification

Notification to affected individuals must include:

| Element                                | Description                                                    |
|----------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Description of the breach**          | What happened, when, and what information was involved         |
| **Steps to reduce risk of harm**       | What the individual can do to protect themselves               |
| **Steps taken by the organisation**    | Actions taken to address the breach                            |
| **Contact information**                | How the individual can get more information or report concerns |
| **Information about complaint rights** | Individuals' right to file a complaint with the OPC            |

### 5.3 Timing of Notification

Notification must be given **as soon as feasible** after the determination that reporting is required. Organisations should not delay notification pending investigation completion if the harm threshold has been met.

---

## 6. Notification to Other Organisations

If the FRFI determines that another organisation could reduce the risk of harm (e.g., a credit bureau, financial institution, law enforcement), the FRFI must notify that organisation as well.

Examples:
- Notifying law enforcement (RCMP, local police) for criminal breaches
- Notifying other FRFIs if fraudulently obtained credentials could be used against accounts at other institutions
- Notifying credit bureaus if financial identity data was breached

---

## 7. Breach Record-Keeping Obligations

### 7.1 Mandatory Record-Keeping for All Breaches

Regardless of whether a breach meets the "real risk of significant harm" threshold, FRFIs must **maintain a record of every breach of security safeguards**.

This is a critical obligation that catches breaches below the reporting threshold.

### 7.2 Minimum Record Content

Records of breaches must contain sufficient information to demonstrate compliance with breach notification obligations. The OPC's guidance suggests records should include:

| Field                         | Description                                                    |
|-------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------|
| Date of breach                | When it was discovered and when it occurred                    |
| Nature of the breach          | How it happened, what systems were involved                    |
| Personal information involved | Type and sensitivity                                           |
| Harm assessment               | Determination of whether real risk of significant harm existed |
| Notification decisions        | Who was notified, when, and why (or why not)                   |
| Remediation actions           | Steps taken to address the breach                              |

### 7.3 Record Retention Period

Breach records must be retained for a **minimum of 24 months** from the date of the breach. The OPC may request access to these records at any time.

### 7.4 OPC Access to Records

Organisations must provide breach records to the OPC upon request — including records for breaches that did not meet the reporting threshold.

---

## 8. Consequences of Non-Compliance

### 8.1 PIPEDA Enforcement

Under PIPEDA, the OPC can:
- Investigate complaints about breach handling
- Issue findings and recommendations
- Apply to Federal Court for an order requiring compliance

### 8.2 Specific Offences Under the DERR

The Regulations create specific **offences** for:
- Knowingly failing to report a breach to the OPC when required
- Knowingly failing to notify affected individuals when required
- Knowingly failing to maintain breach records
- Obstructing the Privacy Commissioner

**Penalties:** Up to **CAD $100,000** per offence (summary conviction).

## 9. Interaction with OSFI Incident Reporting

### 9.1 Same Incident — Two Concurrent Reporting Streams

A single data breach at a FRFI typically triggers **both** PIPEDA DERR reporting obligations and OSFI Advisory incident reporting:

| Obligation        | Trigger                                                                   | Recipient                  | Timeline                                                     |
|-------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------|----------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------|
| **PIPEDA DERR**   | Real risk of significant harm to individuals                              | OPC + affected individuals | As soon as feasible (OPC); as soon as feasible (individuals) |
| **OSFI Advisory** | Any impact or potential impact on FRFI operations or CIA of customer data | OSFI TRD + Lead Supervisor | ≤ 24 hours from detection                                    |

### 9.2 The OSFI Timeline Is Stricter

The OSFI 24-hour reporting window is significantly stricter than PIPEDA's "as soon as feasible" standard. In practice, this means:
- **OSFI notification must happen first** (within 24 hours of detection)
- PIPEDA reporting may follow as the harm assessment is completed
- The timelines are not in conflict — they operate in parallel with different triggers

### 9.3 OPC Notification Triggers OSFI Advisory Criterion C3

Once the FRFI **notifies the OPC** under PIPEDA DERR, this notification triggers **Criterion C3** of the OSFI Advisory (incident reported to regulatory or supervisory agencies). If OPC notification occurs but OSFI has not already been notified, the C3 trigger means OSFI must be notified immediately.

> ⚠️ **Best Practice:** In a breach scenario involving personal information, assume both PIPEDA DERR and the OSFI Advisory are triggered simultaneously and initiate both reporting streams in parallel. Do not sequence them.

## 10. Relevance to OSFI 24-Hour Incident Reporting

### 10.1 Personal Information Breaches are a Primary OSFI Trigger Category

Advisory Criterion **A5** (impact to confidentiality, integrity, or availability of customer information) directly captures privacy breaches. Any incident causing unauthorised access to or disclosure of customer personal information simultaneously triggers:
- OSFI Advisory reporting (24 hours)
- PIPEDA DERR assessment (and reporting if real risk of significant harm)

### 10.2 The "As Soon As Feasible" vs. "24 Hours" Gap

The PIPEDA DERR standard of "as soon as feasible" is more flexible than OSFI's 24-hour window. However:
- The harm assessment required by PIPEDA takes time
- This creates a risk that PIPEDA reporting is perceived as a "slower" obligation — but the OSFI clock is already running from detection
- FRFIs must not wait for the PIPEDA harm assessment to complete before notifying OSFI

### 10.3 Breach Records as OSFI Documentation

PIPEDA DERR's 24-month record-keeping requirement means FRFIs will have documented records of all breaches. These records:
- Provide an audit trail that OSFI may examine during supervisory reviews
- Should cross-reference the OSFI Incident Reporting Form submission
- Demonstrate that the FRFI has an active breach management programme consistent with B-13 P10 (data management)

### 10.4 Potential Transition to CPPA

If Bill C-27's Consumer Privacy Protection Act (CPPA) is enacted, PIPEDA DERR would be superseded by the CPPA's breach reporting provisions. The CPPA is expected to maintain a similar "real risk of significant harm" threshold but with an expanded scope of harm and strengthened enforcement. Until CPPA is enacted, PIPEDA DERR remains in full force.

> 📌 **Summary:** PIPEDA DERR's breach reporting obligations are directly linked to OSFI incident reporting through (1) the A5 trigger criterion (customer information breach), (2) the C3 trigger (OPC notification), and (3) the 24-month record-keeping obligation that provides an audit trail for OSFI supervisory review. FRFIs must run both reporting streams concurrently for personal information breaches.
