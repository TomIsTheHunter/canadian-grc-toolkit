## 1. Overview

The OSFI Corporate Governance Guideline establishes OSFI's expectations for the **governance of federally regulated financial institutions** — specifically the roles, responsibilities, and accountability structures for the **Board of Directors** and **Senior Management**.

Good corporate governance is the foundation of sound risk management. The guideline reflects OSFI's view that strong governance — including clear accountability, robust oversight, and an effective risk culture — is a prerequisite for managing the complex risks facing modern financial institutions, including technology and cyber risks.

### Core Governance Principles

- **Board accountability:** The Board is ultimately accountable for the prudent and sound management of the FRFI
- **Active oversight:** The Board must actively oversee, not merely ratify, management decisions
- **Independent judgment:** Board members must exercise independent judgment in the interests of the FRFI
- **Risk appetite:** The Board owns the risk appetite framework and must ensure the FRFI operates within it
- **Culture:** The Board sets the tone from the top and is accountable for risk culture

## 2. Board of Directors — Composition and Responsibilities

### 2.1 Composition Expectations

| Expectation      | Description                                                                                                                       |
|------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Independence** | A majority of Board members should be independent of management and free from material conflicts of interest                      |
| **Competence**   | Collectively, the Board must have expertise spanning finance, risk management, technology, legal/compliance, and sector knowledge |
| **Diversity**    | OSFI encourages diversity of background, experience, and perspective on the Board                                                 |
| **Renewal**      | Planned Board renewal to avoid entrenchment; term limits and succession planning                                                  |

### 2.2 Core Board Responsibilities

The Board is responsible for:

- **Strategic direction:** Approving the strategic plan and overseeing its execution
- **Risk appetite:** Approving the Risk Appetite Statement (RAS) and ensuring the FRFI operates within it
- **Capital and liquidity:** Approving capital management and liquidity policies
- **Senior Management oversight:** Approving the appointment and compensation of key executives; evaluating CEO performance
- **Culture and conduct:** Setting ethical standards and overseeing culture and conduct programmes
- **Material risk oversight:** Actively overseeing the FRFI's material risks, including technology and cyber risk

### 2.3 Technology and Cyber Risk Oversight

The Corporate Governance Guideline, read with B-13, requires the Board to:
- Receive regular reporting on technology and cyber risk
- Approve the technology and cyber risk appetite
- Be briefed on material technology and cybersecurity incidents
- Oversee the FRFI's technology and cybersecurity posture through a Board Risk Committee or equivalent

## 3. Senior Management Responsibilities

### 3.1 Role of Senior Management

**Senior Management** (the CEO and direct reports, including CRO, CFO, CIO, CISO, CCO, CLO) is responsible for:

- Implementing the strategic direction approved by the Board
- Operating the FRFI within the Board-approved risk appetite
- Maintaining effective risk management frameworks and controls
- Providing the Board with timely, accurate, and complete information to enable effective oversight
- Managing day-to-day operations and responding to emerging risks

### 3.2 Senior Management Accountability for Technology Risk

Under B-13 and the Corporate Governance Guideline together:
- The **CIO / Chief Technology Officer** is accountable for technology operations and resilience
- The **CISO / Chief Information Security Officer** is accountable for cybersecurity
- The **CRO / Chief Risk Officer** is accountable for the second-line risk management framework, including technology risk

These roles must escalate material technology and cyber risk issues to the Board through structured reporting and — in the case of significant incidents — through immediate briefings.

## 4. Risk Governance and Oversight

### 4.1 Three Lines of Defence

The Corporate Governance Guideline reinforces the **three lines of defence** model:

| Line            | Function                                 | Technology Risk Application                                                                                  |
|-----------------|------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **First Line**  | Business lines and technology operations | Day-to-day management of technology and cyber controls; incident detection and initial response              |
| **Second Line** | Risk management and compliance           | Technology risk oversight; policy governance; CISO/SOC functions                                             |
| **Third Line**  | Internal audit                           | Independent assurance over technology risk framework; audit of B-13 compliance; incident response capability |

### 4.2 Risk Appetite Statement (RAS)

The Board must approve a **Risk Appetite Statement** that:
- Articulates the FRFI's tolerance for technology and cyber risk (quantitative and qualitative)
- Establishes Key Risk Indicators (KRIs) and thresholds
- Is reviewed and updated at least annually or following material changes

> 📌 **Incident Reporting Trigger D4:** A breach of RAS thresholds or technology KRIs is an explicit OSFI incident reporting trigger. The Board's RAS directly creates the trigger criteria.

### 4.3 Board Reporting on Technology Risk

Boards should receive regular reporting (at minimum quarterly, more frequently during material incidents) covering:
- Technology and cyber KRI status relative to appetite
- Material vulnerabilities and remediation progress
- Third-party technology risk concentration
- Status of outstanding B-13, B-10, and I&S action items
- Significant security incidents and near-misses

## 5. Audit and Risk Committee Functions

### 5.1 Audit Committee

| Responsibility                        | Technology Risk Dimension                                               |
|---------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Financial reporting oversight         | Ensuring IT general controls (ITGCs) are in place for financial systems |
| External and internal audit oversight | Technology and cyber audits; IAfunction independence                    |
| Control environment oversight         | Access controls, change management, IT governance                       |

### 5.2 Risk Committee

| Responsibility               | Technology Risk Dimension                                            |
|------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Risk appetite oversight      | Technology and cyber risk appetite approval and monitoring           |
| Material risk reporting      | Receiving and challenging technology risk reports                    |
| B-13 / B-10 / E-21 oversight | Ensuring the FRFI's compliance posture relative to OSFI expectations |
| Incident briefings           | Receiving briefings on significant technology and cyber incidents    |

### 5.3 Audit Committee / Risk Committee Coordination

For significant technology incidents, both committees may need to be briefed — the Risk Committee on the risk implications, the Audit Committee on control failures and financial statement impacts. Clear governance protocols should define which committee is briefed in what circumstances.

## 6. Culture, Conduct, and Ethical Standards

The Corporate Governance Guideline places significant emphasis on **risk culture** as a governance outcome:

### 6.1 Risk Culture Dimensions

| Dimension              | Technology Risk Application                                                         |
|------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Tone from the top**  | Board and Executive commitment to cybersecurity as a business priority              |
| **Speak up culture**   | Employees empowered to raise technology and security concerns without fear          |
| **Accountability**     | Clear ownership of technology risks; no ambiguity about who is responsible for what |
| **Risk awareness**     | Employees trained on phishing, social engineering, and security hygiene             |
| **Escalation culture** | Incidents escalated promptly; no incentive to minimise or delay                     |

### 6.2 Impact on Incident Reporting

A strong risk culture that rewards rapid escalation directly supports the OSFI 24-hour reporting obligation. Conversely, a culture where incidents are "managed quietly" or where there are incentives to minimise the severity of issues increases the risk of non-reporting or late reporting.

## 7. Escalation Expectations During Incidents

### 7.1 Board Notification in Crisis Scenarios

The Corporate Governance Guideline creates an expectation that the Board is **notified promptly** during a significant crisis or incident:

- The **Board Chair** and key committee chairs (Risk Committee, Audit Committee) should be notified of P1/critical incidents as soon as practically possible
- The full Board or relevant committee should be formally briefed at the earliest practicable opportunity
- Board members should receive sufficient information to discharge their oversight responsibilities

### 7.2 Escalation Hierarchy During an Incident

```
Detection / Incident Declared
        │
        ▼
First-Line Response (IT/Security)
        │
        ▼
CISO / CIO — Incident Command
        │
        ├─────────────────────────────────────────┐
        │                                         │
        ▼                                         ▼
CEO / CRO / General Counsel            CFO (if financial impact)
        │                                         │
        └────────────────┬────────────────────────┘
                         │
                         ▼
              Board Chair / Risk Committee Chair
                         │
                         ▼
               Full Board Briefing (as required)
```

### 7.3 The "Need to Know" Principle at Board Level

Board members must receive information about material incidents even if the incident response is ongoing. The governance guideline's active oversight expectation means Boards cannot be insulated from bad news — the management obligation is to keep the Board informed, not to shield them from difficult information.

## 8. Governance Structures Intersecting with Incident Reporting Triggers

Several governance structures established by the Corporate Governance Guideline directly intersect with OSFI incident reporting trigger criteria:

| Governance Structure                          | Trigger Criterion                                                    |
|-----------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Board notification procedures**             | C2 — Incident reported to Board or Senior/Executive Management       |
| **Risk Appetite Statement thresholds**        | D4 — Breach of internal risk appetite or thresholds                  |
| **Board-approved severity classification**    | D1 — FRFI-assessed high/critical severity (P1/P2)                    |
| **Concurrent regulatory reporting protocols** | C3 — Reported to OPC and/or other regulatory or supervisory agencies |
| **Insurance claims governance**               | C4 — Cyber insurance claim initiated                                 |
| **Board-level crisis communication plans**    | D3 — Negative reputational impact is imminent                        |

## 9. Relevance to OSFI 24-Hour Incident Reporting

### 9.1 Board Escalation is a Direct Reporting Trigger

The single most direct connection between the Corporate Governance Guideline and the OSFI Incident Reporting Advisory is **Criterion C2**:

> **C2 — Incident reported to the Board of Directors or Senior/Executive Management** is an explicit, standalone trigger for OSFI reporting.

The governance structures required by this guideline — including mandatory Board briefing for material incidents — directly generate this trigger. When a FRFI's governance protocols dictate a Board briefing, OSFI notification must happen concurrently (not after).

### 9.2 Risk Appetite Breaches Create Trigger D4

The Board's Risk Appetite Statement creates the KRI thresholds whose breach is Criterion D4. FRFIs must ensure their technology and cyber KRI thresholds are:
- Set at levels that provide early warning (not just catastrophic thresholds)
- Monitored in near real-time during incidents
- Connected to the incident response and OSFI notification process

### 9.3 Culture Drives Compliance

The OSFI Incident Reporting obligation is, in part, a **cultural compliance requirement**. Non-reporting frequently occurs not because policies are absent but because organisational culture creates incentives to manage incidents internally. The Corporate Governance Guideline's culture expectations — if implemented — directly mitigate this risk.

### 9.4 Senior Management Accountability for Reporting

Senior Management (specifically the CISO and CRO) is accountable for ensuring OSFI reporting obligations are met. The Corporate Governance Guideline establishes that Senior Management accountability for regulatory compliance runs directly to the CEO and Board. Non-reporting or late reporting is a governance failure, not merely a process failure.

> 📌 **Summary:** The Corporate Governance Guideline creates the accountability structures, escalation protocols, and risk appetite thresholds that directly activate multiple OSFI incident reporting trigger criteria. Board-level escalation (C2) and risk appetite breaches (D4) are governance outcomes that **require simultaneous OSFI notification**.
